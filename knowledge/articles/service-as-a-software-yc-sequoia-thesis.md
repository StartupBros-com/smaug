# Service as a Software

YC's Summer 2026 RFS explicitly asks founders to build companies that *replace* services rather than assist them — "AI-native companies that don't sell software, they sell the service." Sequoia frames the opportunity with a 1:6 ratio: for every dollar spent on software, six go to the people delivering the service that software supports. Service as a Software is the first model that lets one company credibly target all seven dollars in workflows AI can fully own.

## The Economic Thesis

- **Sequoia's 1:6 ratio** — SaaS captured the $1. Operators captured the $6. The new model targets both.
- **Garry Tan's 16 verticals** — healthcare, legal, finance, insurance, compliance, logistics, customer service, and more, each at under 5% AI penetration today.
- **Price compression** — in early wins, outcome price has dropped from human-services pricing toward software pricing (tax returns, claim reviews).

## Three Service Layers

1. **Production work** — repeatable execution (filing returns, drafting contracts). Being eaten first and fastest.
2. **Pattern application** — translating known problems with industry playbooks. Next target; speed varies by vertical cleanliness.
3. **Strategic direction** — picking the right problem, framing the engagement. Not being absorbed; becoming more valuable as execution costs collapse.

## Five Signals a Service Is Ripe

1. Work is repeatable (same shape, different inputs)
2. Already outsourced (customer accepts third-party execution)
3. Clear right answer or tight acceptable band
4. Customer measures success by outcome, not effort
5. Margins high enough to attract a software-priced alternative

## Risks and Counter-Evidence

Enterprise surveys (EIOPA GenAI report, McKinsey State of AI 2025) consistently flag human oversight requirements, hallucinations, and trust as binding constraints in regulated verticals. The bull case (full 1:6 capture) is the upper bound, not the central forecast. Winners will own distribution, domain expertise, workflow integration, risk controls, and trust — not just model quality.

## Key Frame

> "The two failure modes are equally expensive. Treat the shift as inevitable and you overpay for an early position. Treat it as overblown and you miss the only structural reset business software has had in twenty years."
