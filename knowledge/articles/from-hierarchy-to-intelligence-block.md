A Sequoia-authored piece about Block's radical organizational redesign: replacing the human hierarchy as an information routing mechanism with an AI-powered "world model" of the company. Traces organizational design from the Roman army through military hierarchy, railroads, scientific management, and matrix orgs — all as variations on the same coordination bottleneck. Argues that AI is the first technology capable of actually performing what hierarchy does (compress and route information), enabling a fundamentally different company structure.

The core thesis: output scales linearly with headcount, but coordination cost scales exponentially. Middle management exists not to produce anything but to compress and relay information — a lossy, high-latency, low-bandwidth protocol. Block intends to replace the hierarchy entirely with two world models: a company world model (live understanding of operations, priorities, decisions) and a customer world model (per-customer financial behavior from both sides of transactions via Cash App + Square).

## Key Takeaways

- Hierarchy is fundamentally a compression algorithm for organizational knowledge — lossy and high-latency by design
- At 10,000 people, ~60% of headcount is coordination overhead, not production
- Block's model normalizes to three roles: Individual Contributors (deep specialists), Directly Responsible Individuals (DRIs who own cross-cutting problems for a defined window), and Player-Coaches (builders who also develop people — not information routers)
- The intelligence layer composes atomic financial capabilities into solutions proactively, without a product manager deciding what to build; failure signals (when the layer can't compose a solution) become the roadmap
- Money is the "most honest signal in the world" — Block sees both sides of millions of transactions daily, compounding the customer world model
- The shift from "give agents memory" to "give agents organizational judgment" defines the next platform war
- Context graphs — living representations of organizational decision traces — will be to the 2030s what databases were to the 2000s
